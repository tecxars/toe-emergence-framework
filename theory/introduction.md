# High‑Level Concept: A QCA on a Dynamical Quantized Graph

The core idea is to model the fundamental layer of the universe as an information‑theoretic system rather than a geometric one. Instead of assuming space, time, or continuity, the model begins with a discrete set of informational units connected by dynamically evolving relations. These units form a quantized graph whose nodes and edges change over discrete time steps.

On this evolving graph, local quantum operations act as update rules. Each update is unitary and applies only to the finite neighborhood defined by the current edge structure. Because the graph itself changes over time, the notion of locality is not fixed but dynamically determined. This produces a quantum cellular automaton whose causal structure is encoded directly in the pattern of allowed local updates.

All physical content is represented within a C*-algebraic framework: each finite region of the graph carries a local C*-algebra of observables, and the global algebra arises as their inductive limit. The unitary updates define automorphisms of this algebra, ensuring that the evolution is consistent, reversible, and respects causality at the algebraic level.

In this view, geometry, dimension, and spacetime are not fundamental ingredients. They emerge from the interplay between the evolving graph structure and the algebraic dynamics. The model provides a purely discrete, information‑based foundation from which higher‑level physical concepts can arise.

## Scope and Related Research Areas

This framework connects to several active and partially overlapping research areas in theoretical and mathematical physics. The approach does not belong to a single established field but combines concepts from multiple domains in a way that is not present in existing literature. The most relevant areas are:

Quantum Cellular Automata (QCA):
The model is based on discrete, local, unitary update rules that define the fundamental dynamics. Traditional QCA models operate on fixed lattices with static neighborhoods. In contrast, this framework generalizes the concept to a setting where adjacency relations can evolve, making the QCA itself dynamic.

Emergent Spacetime:
The framework assumes that spacetime is not fundamental. Instead, geometric and causal structure arise from the evolution of a quantized graph. This places the project within the broader context of emergent spacetime research, where geometry is derived rather than postulated. Related approaches include Wheeler’s “It from Bit”, Lloyd’s computational universe, and various discrete spacetime models.

Dynamical Graph Models:
The underlying graph is not fixed. Nodes and edges can change according to local rules. This connects the project to approaches where topology is dynamical, such as quantum graphity and spin‑network‑based models. However, unlike existing approaches, the present framework uses a strictly unitary quantum update rule rather than classical, stochastic, or constraint‑driven dynamics.

C*-Algebraic Quantum Theory:
Local observables are encoded in C*-algebras associated with nodes or neighborhoods of the graph. This provides a mathematically rigorous foundation and connects the model to algebraic quantum field theory, operator algebras, and the study of local quantum systems. The use of C*-algebras also aligns the framework with Haag–Kastler axiomatic structures and modern operator‑algebraic approaches to quantum systems.

Quantum Information Theory:
The universe is treated as an information‑processing structure. Concepts such as locality, unitarity, entanglement, and computational structure play a central role. This aligns the project with information‑theoretic perspectives on physics, including work by Landauer, Bennett, Lloyd, and others who emphasize that physical laws may emerge from constraints on information processing.

Discrete Approaches to Field Theory:
Fields, particles, and interactions are not fundamental but emerge from the microscopic update rules of the graph. This relates the framework to discrete models of quantum field theory, lattice approaches, tensor‑network‑based constructions, and attempts to derive continuum physics from underlying discrete structures.

Foundations of Quantum Mechanics:
Because the model is built from local unitary rules on a discrete structure, it touches on foundational questions such as the origin of quantum behavior, the role of locality, and the emergence of effective continuum descriptions. This connects to research by ’t Hooft, Hardy, and others exploring deterministic or discrete substructures underlying quantum theory.

Information-Theoretic and Physical Limits on Continuity:
Several established principles support the assumption that the universe cannot store or manipulate exact continuous values. Landauer’s principle states that information is physical and that storing or erasing information requires finite energy, implying that any physical system can only encode a finite number of distinguishable states. The Bekenstein bound limits the maximum information content of any finite region of space, making infinitely precise continuous degrees of freedom physically implausible. Quantum theory reinforces this perspective: systems with finite energy have finite-dimensional effective state spaces, and measurement outcomes are inherently discrete. Continuous fields and continuous spacetime therefore appear best understood as effective approximations emerging from discrete underlying dynamics rather than as fundamental structures.

This combination of QCA dynamics, quantized evolving graphs, operator‑algebraic structure, and information‑theoretic constraints does not appear in publicly available models and represents, to the best of my knowledge, a novel direction for exploring the emergence of spacetime, matter, and interactions.
